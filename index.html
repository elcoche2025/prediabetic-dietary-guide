Evidence-Based Nutrition

Your Prediabetes
Dietary Guide

Practical, science-backed nutrition strategies to help manage blood sugar and reduce your risk of developing type 2 diabetes.

This guide is for general educational purposes only. It is not medical advice. Always consult your doctor, endocrinologist, or registered dietitian before making dietary changes — especially if you take medication for blood sugar management.

Core Dietary Principles

These six foundational strategies help keep blood sugar stable and support metabolic health.

🥬

Prioritize Fiber

Aim for 25–35g of fiber daily. Fiber slows glucose absorption, improves insulin sensitivity, and keeps you full longer. Vegetables, legumes, and whole grains are excellent sources.

🐟

Choose Lean Proteins

Include protein at every meal — it stabilizes blood sugar and reduces post-meal spikes. Fish, chicken, eggs, beans, and tofu are great options.

🫒

Embrace Healthy Fats

Unsaturated fats from olive oil, avocados, nuts, and fatty fish support heart health and slow digestion, helping to moderate blood sugar responses.

🚫

Limit Refined Carbs

White bread, pastries, sugary drinks, and processed snacks cause rapid blood sugar spikes. Replace them with whole, unprocessed alternatives whenever possible.

⏰

Eat Consistently

Regular meal timing prevents extreme blood sugar swings. Avoid skipping meals, and consider balanced snacks between meals if needed to maintain stable levels.

💧

Stay Hydrated

Water is your best beverage. Dehydration can concentrate blood sugar. Aim for 8+ cups daily. Unsweetened tea and coffee in moderation are also fine.

½ Non-Starchy Vegetables

Broccoli, spinach, peppers, salad greens, zucchini, asparagus, green beans. High in fiber and nutrients, very low impact on blood sugar.

¼ Lean Protein

Grilled chicken, fish, tofu, eggs, beans, or lean meat. Protein keeps you satisfied and slows glucose absorption.

¼ Complex Carbohydrates

Brown rice, quinoa, sweet potato, whole grain bread. These provide sustained energy without sharp blood sugar spikes.

🥤 Don't Forget Your Beverage

Pair your plate with water, unsweetened tea, or sparkling water with lemon. Sugary drinks — including fruit juices — can undermine an otherwise balanced meal.

Sample Day of Meals

A realistic example day showing how the principles come together in practice.

Breakfast — 7:30 AM
Veggie Scramble with Avocado
Two eggs scrambled with spinach, mushrooms, and bell peppers. Served with ¼ avocado and a small slice of whole grain toast.
~22g protein ~18g carbs ~20g fat ~6g fiber
Mid-Morning Snack — 10:00 AM
Apple Slices with Almond Butter
Half a medium apple with 1 tablespoon of natural almond butter. The fiber and fat combination helps prevent a glucose spike.
~4g protein ~15g carbs ~9g fat
Lunch — 12:30 PM
Grilled Chicken Salad Bowl
Grilled chicken over a bed of mixed greens, cherry tomatoes, cucumbers, chickpeas, and a drizzle of olive oil and lemon dressing. Served with ½ cup of quinoa.
~35g protein ~32g carbs ~16g fat ~9g fiber
Afternoon Snack — 3:00 PM
Handful of Walnuts + Blueberries
A small handful of walnuts (~14 halves) paired with ½ cup of fresh blueberries. Heart-healthy omega-3s and antioxidants.
~5g protein ~14g carbs ~13g fat
Dinner — 6:30 PM
Baked Salmon with Roasted Vegetables
A filet of salmon baked with herbs, alongside roasted broccoli and asparagus, with a small side of brown rice (½ cup cooked).
~34g protein ~28g carbs ~18g fat ~7g fiber
Evening — 8:00 PM (Optional)
Herbal Tea + Small Cottage Cheese
A cup of chamomile or peppermint tea with ½ cup of low-fat cottage cheese. A light, protein-rich option if you need something before bed.
~14g protein ~5g carbs ~2g fat

Lifestyle Tips

Diet is the foundation — these habits amplify its effectiveness.

1

Walk After Meals

Even a 10–15 minute walk after eating can significantly reduce post-meal blood sugar spikes. It's one of the simplest, most effective habits you can build.

2

Eat Vegetables First

Starting your meal with non-starchy vegetables or protein before carbohydrates has been shown to reduce glucose and insulin spikes compared to eating carbs first.

3

Prioritize Sleep

Poor sleep increases insulin resistance and hunger hormones. Aim for 7–9 hours of quality sleep. Consistent bedtimes and limiting screen time before bed help.

4

Read Nutrition Labels

Check for added sugars (listed under many names like dextrose, maltose, and high-fructose corn syrup), total carbohydrates, and fiber content. High fiber relative to carbs is a good sign.

5

Manage Stress

Chronic stress raises cortisol, which increases blood sugar. Regular stress reduction — whether through exercise, meditation, hobbies, or socializing — supports better glucose control.

6

Pair Carbs with Protein or Fat

Never eat carbs alone. Adding protein, fat, or fiber to carbohydrate-rich foods slows digestion and flattens the blood sugar curve. For example: apple + almond butter, toast + egg.

7

Add Vinegar to Meals

A tablespoon of apple cider vinegar in water before meals has been shown in studies to modestly improve insulin sensitivity and lower post-meal glucose.

8

Build Muscle

Resistance training (bodyweight exercises, weights, resistance bands) increases the amount of glucose your muscles absorb. Even 2–3 sessions per week makes a measurable difference.

Prediabetes is reversible for many people through sustained dietary and lifestyle changes. Small, consistent steps matter more than perfection. Work with your healthcare team to find an approach that fits your life.
